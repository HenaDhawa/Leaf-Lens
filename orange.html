Introduction

Orange is a popular and nutritious citrus fruit that is widely consumed globally, with a significant portion of production being used for fresh market, juice, and other processed products. Orange is one of the top 10 most produced fruits worldwide.

Care

Orange trees can be cultivated using various methods, including grafting and budding. When planting, trees should be spaced to ensure adequate air circulation and sunlight penetration, with a recommended distance of 6-10 meters between trees. Orange trees are evergreen and require consistent moisture, especially during the fruiting stage. Regular pruning is essential for maintaining tree vitality and promoting a strong framework. Thinning fruits (to about 2-3 fruits per branch) is also crucial for achieving optimal fruit size and quality.



Soil

Well-drained, fertile soils with a pH of 6.0-6.5 are ideal for orange tree propagation. Soils should not be water-logged or have compacted subsoil. Adding organic matter like compost or manure can help retain soil moisture and provide essential nutrients. Orange trees are heavy feeders and benefit from regular fertilization.

Climate

Orange trees are subtropical and thrive in temperatures between 12 and 30°C. They can be grown in a variety of climates, but optimal growth occurs in regions with mild winters and long, warm summers. Adequate moisture, especially during the fruiting stage, is essential for orange tree growth. Full sun and warm, sunny conditions help produce fruits with high sugar content and a longer shelf life. Wind protection is also necessary, as strong winds can damage trees and reduce fruit quality.

Orange Diseases

Orange Haunglongbing(Citrus greening)

Huanglongbing, commonly known as citrus greening, is a devastating bacterial disease affecting citrus trees worldwide. It poses a significant threat to the citrus industry, causing substantial economic losses.

citrus greening is caused by a bacterium, not a fungus. Symptoms include yellowing of leaves, premature fruit drop, and bitter, green-colored fruit.
The disease is spread primarily by the Asian citrus psyllid, a tiny insect that feeds on citrus plants. Once a tree is infected, there is currently no cure.
Citrus greening thrives in warm climates and can impact all types of citrus trees, including oranges, grapefruits, lemons, and limes.
